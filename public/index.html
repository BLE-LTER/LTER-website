We study the seasons where land meets sea at the top of the world

Beaufort Lagoon Ecosystems LTER is a new Long Term Ecological Research* site. We use diverse methods to study lagoons and other aquatic sites along the northern Alaskan Arctic coast. Our interests include land/sea interactions in these unique ecosystems, their seasonal dynamics, their biological communities, the way the lagoons are changing, and using our expertise to serve local communities.



* We joined the U.S. LTER network in 2017. The newest and northernmost in the 28-site network, BLE LTER will address gaps in our understanding of the rapidly changing Arctic. We collaborate closely with other sites to do the best science and to promote open research.

Our study sites

Coastal lagoons are among the most productive habitats and make up more than 10% of the land-sea interface worldwide. The Beaufort Sea lagoons are unique, dynamic, and fragile ecosystems. These systems encompass more than half of the Beaufort Sea coast, providing food and habitat for large populations of migratory fish and waterfowl essential to the culture of northern Alaska's Inupiaq communities. Dozens of rivers, a.k.a the 'highways of Alaska' and altogether make a vast drainage basin, flow into the Beaufort Sea through these lagoons.

Seasons in the lagoons

Dramatic seasonality is a big reason why we think the lagoons are such interesting places to study. Seasons also influence everything we do in terms of logistics. BLE goes all out on field work three times a year; each season brings its own challenges and rewards.

Frozen over

In winter, freshwater trickles from the many North Slope rivers make up the primary source of input to the lagoons. We sample the lagoons in April to represent winter conditions.

Ice break-up

Spring comes very rapidly to the Arctic once daylight lengthens. Ice break-up can happen in a matter of days. We sample the lagoons in June to represent spring conditions.

Open water

In summer, the lagoons exchange materials with the Beaufort Sea freely. Ecosystem productivity peaks around this time. We sample in August to represent summer conditions.

Opportunity: Summer 2020 Undergraduate Research Experience

We are seeking an undergraduate researcher interested in aquatic coastal ecology, Arctic ecology, environmental science, biogeochemistry, or related topics, to join the Beaufort Lagoon Ecosystems project under the NSF Research Experience for Undergraduates (REU) program.

Program information

  • Location: the University of Texas at El Paso (El Paso, TX) or the University of Texas at Austin, Marine Science Institute (Port Aransas, TX).
  • Duration: 11 weeks; tentative dates are from June 1st to August 14th 2020.
  • Opportunity to travel to northern Alaska during this time with the BLE LTER team to conduct field work for 2-3 weeks.
  • Complete a research project under mentorship from a BLE LTER scientist.
  • $5,500 stipend.

Eligibility requirements

  • U.S. citizen or permanent resident
  • Full time availability for the duration of the program
  • 3.0 minimum GPA
  • Full time undergraduate student studying Biology, Environmental Science, or associated field
  • Expected graduation date on or after December 2020

How to apply

For more information or to apply, email Dr. Vanessa Lougheed at vlougheed@utep.edu. Deadline to apply is February 15th, 2020.

The BLE LTER project actively participates in K-12 Programs in Kaktovik and Utqiagvik through formal connections and activities with school teachers and administrators of the North Slope Borough School District. These partnerships connect BLE LTER affiliated students with K-12 students living in North Slope villages through the Research Experiences for Undergraduates (REU) program. Such partnerships benefit enormously from nurturing a culture of diversity. We encourage applications from potential REU students that are traditionally underrepresented in the range of disciplines represented by our research team.
